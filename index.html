Beyond Mendel: Non-Mendelian Inheritance

What is Non-Mendelian Genetics?

While Mendel's laws form the foundation of genetics, many traits don't follow simple dominant/recessive patterns. Non-Mendelian inheritance describes inheritance patterns where alleles don't show complete dominance, or where multiple alleles exist for a single gene.

Mendelian vs. Non-Mendelian Inheritance

Key Distinction: Codominance vs. Incomplete Dominance

  • Codominance: Both phenotypes appear TOGETHER (side by side) - like red AND white hairs in roan cattle
  • Incomplete Dominance: A BLENDED intermediate phenotype appears - like pink (a mix of red and white)

Think of it this way: Codominance is like a checkerboard (both colors visible), while incomplete dominance is like mixing paint (new color).

Codominance

Definition

Codominance occurs when both alleles in a heterozygote are fully and separately expressed. Neither allele is dominant or recessive - both contribute equally to the phenotype, and both phenotypes are visible simultaneously.

Classic Example: Roan Cattle

Another Example: Sickle Cell Anemia

Hemoglobin in Humans

Sickle cell anemia demonstrates codominance at the molecular level:

Genotype Hemoglobin Type Phenotype
HAHA 100% normal hemoglobin (HbA) Normal red blood cells
HAHS ~50% HbA and ~50% HbS Sickle cell trait (both types present)
HSHS 100% sickle hemoglobin (HbS) Sickle cell disease

Blood Smear from Heterozygote (HAHS)

Shows both normal (round) and sickle-shaped red blood cells

Codominance at molecular level: Heterozygotes produce BOTH normal AND sickle hemoglobin proteins simultaneously - this is codominance!

Note: At the whole-organism level, sickle cell can also be viewed as incomplete dominance, since heterozygotes have an intermediate disease severity.

Heterozygote Advantage: Why Does the Sickle Cell Allele Persist?

Sickle cell disease (HSHS) is devastating - it causes severe anemia, pain crises, organ damage, and shortened lifespan. So why hasn't natural selection eliminated the HS allele from the population?

The answer: Heterozygote advantage (also called balancing selection)

The Malaria Connection

Individuals who are heterozygous (HAHS) have a significant survival advantage in regions where malaria is endemic:

Genotype Sickle Cell Status Malaria Resistance Overall Fitness
HAHA No sickle cells - healthy Susceptible to malaria Moderate (malaria risk)
HAHS Mild anemia (sickle cell trait) Resistant to malaria Highest
HSHS Severe sickle cell disease Resistant to malaria Low (disease effects)

How Does It Work?

  • The malaria parasite (Plasmodium falciparum) infects and reproduces inside red blood cells
  • In heterozygotes, when the parasite infects a cell, it causes that cell to sickle
  • Sickled cells are destroyed by the spleen, killing the parasite inside before it can reproduce
  • This provides significant protection against severe malaria without the full effects of sickle cell disease

Geographic Evidence

The frequency of the HS allele is highest (up to 20%) in regions of Africa, the Mediterranean, and South Asia where malaria has historically been endemic. This geographic correlation strongly supports the heterozygote advantage hypothesis.

This is natural selection in action: In malaria-endemic regions, heterozygotes survive and reproduce at higher rates than either homozygote, maintaining both alleles in the population.

Codominance Notation

In codominance, we typically use superscripts to denote different alleles since neither is dominant or recessive:

  • CR and CW for cattle coat color
  • IA and IB for blood type alleles
  • HA and HS for hemoglobin types

This notation shows that both alleles are equal in their expression.

Why? If you receive blood with antigens your body doesn't recognize, your antibodies will attack those red blood cells, causing a potentially fatal transfusion reaction.

Paternity Testing with Blood Types

Blood types can be used to exclude possible parents (but not confirm them):

  • A child with Type AB cannot have a Type O parent
  • Two Type O parents can only have Type O children
  • A Type O child must receive an i allele from each parent

Incomplete Dominance

Definition

Incomplete dominance (also called partial dominance or semi-dominance) occurs when the heterozygote shows a phenotype that is intermediate between the two homozygotes. The dominant allele doesn't completely mask the recessive allele.

Molecular Explanation

Why Does This Happen?

Incomplete dominance typically occurs due to gene dosage effects:

  • RR: Two functional alleles → produces 100% of the pigment enzyme → full red color
  • Rr: One functional allele → produces 50% of the pigment enzyme → intermediate (pink) color
  • rr: No functional alleles → produces 0% of the pigment enzyme → no color (white)

The r allele doesn't produce functional enzyme, so heterozygotes only have half the normal amount of pigment.

Other Examples of Incomplete Dominance

Feather Color in Chickens

Black × White = Blue (gray) feathers

The gray color is a true blend, not a mixture of black and white feathers.

Hair Texture in Humans

Curly × Straight = Wavy hair

This is a simplified model; actual hair texture is polygenic.

Tay-Sachs Disease

At the enzyme level, heterozygotes have ~50% of normal enzyme activity - an intermediate level.

Lethal Alleles

Definition

Lethal alleles are alleles that cause death when present in certain combinations (usually homozygous). These alleles alter the expected Mendelian ratios because some offspring don't survive.

Key Concept: Modified Ratios

The Telltale Sign of a Lethal Allele

When you see a 2:1 phenotypic ratio instead of the expected 3:1 from a monohybrid cross, suspect a lethal allele!

Expected 3:1 → Observed 2:1

The "missing" class represents the homozygous lethal genotype that doesn't survive.

Example 3: Huntington's Disease in Humans

A Late-Acting Dominant Lethal

Huntington's disease (HD) is caused by a dominant allele (H) that leads to progressive neurodegeneration. While technically lethal, it's unique because symptoms don't appear until adulthood (typically age 30-50), after reproductive age.

Genotype Phenotype Notes
HH Huntington's disease (severe) Earlier onset, more severe symptoms
Hh Huntington's disease Develops disease (typically age 30-50)
hh Normal Does not develop HD

Why Does a Lethal Allele Persist?

  • Late onset: Symptoms appear after typical reproductive age, so the allele is often passed on before the person knows they have it
  • Dominant: Heterozygotes are affected, unlike recessive lethal alleles which can "hide" in carriers
  • New mutations: The HD gene has a high mutation rate due to its repetitive DNA sequence

Genetic Testing Dilemma

Genetic testing can determine if someone carries the HD allele. This presents ethical questions:

  • Would you want to know if you'll develop an incurable disease?
  • How might this affect family planning decisions?
  • Should genetic information affect insurance or employment?

Types of Lethal Alleles

Recessive Lethals

Only lethal in homozygous state (aa).

Examples: Tay-Sachs, Cystic Fibrosis (without treatment)

Heterozygotes (Aa) are carriers - healthy but can pass on the allele.

Dominant Lethals

Lethal even in heterozygous state.

Examples: Usually don't persist unless late-acting (like Huntington's)

Immediate dominant lethals are eliminated from the population immediately.

Conditional Lethals

Lethal only under certain environmental conditions.

Examples: Some temperature-sensitive mutations

May be viable under normal conditions but lethal under stress.

Pleiotropy Connection

Many lethal alleles demonstrate pleiotropy - one gene affecting multiple traits. For example, the yellow mouse allele (AY) affects:

  • Coat color (yellow instead of agouti)
  • Body size (obesity in heterozygotes)
  • Embryonic development (lethal in homozygotes)

The gene affects multiple body systems because it's involved in a fundamental developmental pathway.
